Reading Passage 1

Base Erosion and Profit Shifting – does the corporate world avoid paying its fair share of tax?

A recent report by Oxfam suggests that statistics in New Zealand show that a list of 20 of the top multi-national earners in New Zealand reported an average profit of just 1.3 per cent for New Zealand-generated revenue, significantly lower than the 5 per cent average for domestically-owned businesses. This suggests that these companies are paying less tax than they should be.

The practice of avoiding tax is nothing new. However, in recent years, the scale of the problem has become much more apparent. Many large multinational companies have been accused of using complex corporate structures to shift profits to low-tax jurisdictions, thereby reducing their overall tax burden.

This phenomenon, known as Base Erosion and Profit Shifting (BEPS), has become a major concern for governments worldwide. The OECD estimates that BEPS costs countries between $100-240 billion in lost revenue annually, or anywhere from 4-10% of global corporate income tax revenues.

One of the most common strategies used by multinational corporations is transfer pricing. This involves setting prices for transactions between different parts of the same company in different countries in such a way as to minimize tax liability. For example, a subsidiary in a high-tax country might pay inflated prices for services or intellectual property from a subsidiary in a low-tax country.
